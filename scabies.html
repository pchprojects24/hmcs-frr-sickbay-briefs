Overview What scabies is and how it spreads

Scabies is caused by a tiny mite (Sarcoptes scabiei) that burrows into the skin. The main symptom is intense itching, often worse at night, with a rash or small raised lines in affected areas.

Common sites: between fingers, wrists, waistband, underarms, and groin. The face and scalp are generally not affected in adults.

How it spreads in close quarters
  • Direct, prolonged skin-to-skin contact — casual brief contact (like a handshake) is low risk.
  • Sharing bedding, clothing, or towels with someone who has an active infestation.
  • Close berthing environments increase exposure risk.

Itching may not begin until 2–6 weeks after exposure in first-time cases, so someone may be infectious before they notice symptoms.

Treatment & Steps Assessment, medication, and personal items
  • Report to Sick Bay if you notice unexplained itching or a rash — especially between fingers, wrists, or in skin folds.
  • Diagnosis is made by medical staff. Treatment is a prescription topical cream (permethrin 5%) applied overnight to all skin from the neck down, then washed off in the morning.
  • A second application is usually done 1–2 weeks later.
  • Itching can persist for 2–4 weeks after successful treatment — this does not mean treatment failed.
Laundering and personal items
  • Wash all worn clothing, bedding, and towels on the day of first treatment — hot water (at least 50 °C) and high-heat drying.
  • Items that cannot be washed (e.g. boots, some gear) can be sealed in a plastic bag for 3–4 days; mites do not survive off the body for more than 2–3 days.
  • Do not share towels, clothing, or bedding during treatment.

Close contacts (berthmates) may also be assessed and treated at the same time, even without symptoms, to prevent re-infestation.

Things to Avoid During and after treatment
  • Do not stop treatment early — complete both applications as directed.
  • Do not apply the cream to only the itchy area; it must be applied to all skin from the neck down.
  • Do not assume itching means treatment hasn't worked — post-treatment itch is normal and can last weeks.
  • Do not share personal items (towels, clothing, bedding) until treatment is fully completed.
  • Do not use more cream than directed — more is not better and can cause irritation.
Signs to be aware of
  • Itching that worsens or spreads significantly after treatment is complete — report back to Sick Bay.
  • Open, crusted, or thickened skin — may indicate a different or more extensive presentation.
  • New rash or itching appearing in berthmates days after treatment — report to Sick Bay so contacts can be assessed.
