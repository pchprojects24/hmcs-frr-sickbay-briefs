First Aid / Skin

Minor Cuts & Wound Care

Stop bleeding, rinse well, cover smartly, and know when to get medical help.

Purpose

A quick, ship-friendly routine for small cuts, scrapes, and blisters.

Try this now (60 seconds) Immediate steps
  • Apply firm pressure with clean cloth/gauze to stop bleeding.
  • Rinse under a gentle stream of potable water for 2–5 minutes; use mild soap on nearby skin.
  • Apply a thin layer of plain petroleum jelly or antibiotic ointment if the wound was dirty.
  • Cover with a clean dressing; change if it gets wet or dirty.
Why this is worse at sea
  • Wet decks and gear friction reopen small cuts.
  • Delayed cleaning during evolutions lets grime sit in wounds.
  • Harbour, bilge, or seawater can contaminate and seed infection.
Self-care for the next 24–72 hours Protect and monitor
  • Keep wounds covered; change dressings at least daily or sooner if wet.
  • Use waterproof dressing if spray/immersion is likely; replace once back inside.
  • Check once per watch for redness, swelling, warmth, pus, or foul smell.
  • Plain petroleum jelly is enough for clean minor wounds; antibiotic ointment is optional for dirtier cuts on hands/feet.
Avoid / common mistakes
  • Do not pour hydrogen peroxide, alcohol, or full-strength iodine into the wound—they damage healing tissue.
  • Do not scrub raw tissue or pick scabs.
  • Do not leave wounds open to seawater or dirty gloves/boots.
Seawater or dirty-water exposure
  • Rinse with potable water and mild soap as soon as possible.
  • Re-apply ointment and a clean, waterproof dressing.

If pain, redness, or swelling ramps up quickly after seawater exposure, seek help urgently.

Tetanus Stay current
  • Routine booster: about every 10 years.
  • Dirty wounds: consider booster if it has been more than 5 years.
  • Sick Bay can check your record and advise on boosters.
Go now (urgent / same watch)
  • Bleeding will not stop with 5–10 minutes of firm pressure.
  • Deep, gaping, puncture, crush, or bite wounds.
  • Something stuck in the wound.
  • Rapidly spreading redness, streaks, fever, or feeling very unwell.
Not improving (within 24–72 hours)
  • Redness, warmth, swelling, or pain getting worse after 24–48 hours.
  • Pus, bad smell, or delayed healing.
  • Unsure about tetanus status, especially if wound was dirty.
