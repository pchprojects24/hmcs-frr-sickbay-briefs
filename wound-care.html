Minor Cuts, Scrapes & Wound Care

Quick self-care steps, warning signs, and when to get medical help for small wounds.

Self-care for minor wounds

Small cuts, scrapes, and blisters can usually be handled yourself if bleeding stops with pressure, the wound is shallow, and edges close easily.

Clean with water and mild soap, apply thin petroleum jelly or antibiotic ointment, and cover with a clean dressing. Change daily or when wet. Avoid seawater exposure.

Get help now if: bleeding won't stop, wound is deep/gaping, it's a puncture or bite, you see spreading redness, or signs of infection appear.

‚úÖIs this wound okay for self-care?

You can usually look after it yourself if:

  • Bleeding stops within a few minutes of firm pressure.
  • The cut is small and shallow, edges close easily.
  • It’s a surface scrape or minor blister/chafing.
  • It’s a tiny skin split on a knuckle, fingertip, or heel.
Go to Sick Bay now if:
  • Bleeding won’t stop with 5‚Äì10 minutes of firm pressure.
  • The wound is deep, gaping, or clearly needs stitches.
  • It’s a puncture (nail, fish spine, metal) or any bite.
  • Something is stuck in the wound that you can’t remove.
  • The injury is near the eye, neck, genitals, or over a suspected fracture.
üöøClean the wound

As soon as you can:

  • Wash your hands with soap or sanitizer.
  • Rinse the wound under a gentle stream of potable water for 2‚Äì5 minutes.
  • Use mild soap on the skin around the wound; let water do most of the cleaning.
  • Gently lift out any visible dirt with clean fingers or tweezers if you can see it easily.

Avoid pouring into the wound: hydrogen peroxide, full-strength iodine, alcohol, or strong detergents ‚Äì they damage healing tissue.

Don’t scrub raw tissue or pick at clots or scabs ‚Äì this restarts bleeding and slows healing.

If you still see grit or debris you can’t remove with gentle rinsing, have Sick Bay check it.

üß¥Antiseptics ‚Äì only if really dirty

Most minor wounds don’t need antiseptic solutions once they are well rinsed.

  • If the wound was heavily contaminated and still looks dirty, a one-time brief rinse with a dilute antiseptic (e.g. chlorhexidine 0.05% or povidone-iodine ~1%) is reasonable.
  • Apply briefly, then rinse again with clean water.
  • Avoid repeated soaking or scrubbing with antiseptics ‚Äì this slows healing.
üíäOintment: petroleum jelly vs antibiotic

After cleaning, apply a thin layer over the wound:

  • Plain petroleum jelly ‚Äì good default for clean, low-risk minor wounds.
  • Antibiotic ointment (e.g. Polysporin®) ‚Äì consider for dirtier cuts, hand/foot wounds, or when infection would impact duty.

Stop using any antibiotic ointment if the surrounding skin becomes red and itchy (possible allergy); switch back to plain petroleum jelly.

ü©πDressings & changing them

Covered, slightly moist wounds heal faster and hurt less.

  • Use a Band-Aid for small cuts, or a non-stick pad and tape for larger scrapes.
  • Only use blister / hydrocolloid dressings on clean wounds (scrapes, blisters) under boots or gear.
  • Change standard dressings at least daily, or sooner if wet or dirty.
  • Hydrocolloid dressings can usually stay 2‚Äì5+ days if sealed, dry, and skin around looks normal ‚Äì check once a day, especially at sea.
  • Wrap snug, not tight ‚Äì fingers and toes should stay warm and pink.
üåäSeawater and "dirty" water

Salt water is not a disinfectant.

  • Avoid exposing open wounds to seawater or dirty bilge/harbour water.
  • If a wound does get soaked in seawater, rinse as soon as you can with potable water and mild soap, then re-dress with fresh ointment and a clean bandage.
  • Keep wounds covered with a waterproof dressing if you expect spray or immersion.
After seawater exposure, get urgent help if:
  • Pain or swelling is rapidly worsening.
  • Redness spreads quickly or skin turns purple/blue.
  • Blisters/bullae appear near the wound.
  • You develop fever, chills, or feel very unwell.
üíâTetanus check
  • Routine tetanus booster: about every 10 years.
  • Dirty or major wounds: a booster is usually recommended if it’s been >5 years.
  • See Sick Bay if you don’t know your last tetanus shot, never finished childhood vaccines, or have a dirty wound and last booster was >5 years ago.

Sick Bay can review your record, advise if a booster is needed, and give it if required.

Go now (urgent) - Same watch / immediately

  • Bleeding won’t stop with firm pressure.
  • Deep, gaping, or long cut that may need stitches.
  • Puncture wounds, bites, crush injuries, or amputations.
  • Foreign body stuck in the wound.
  • Red streaks running away from the wound.
  • Fever, chills, or feeling systemically unwell.

Not improving - Within 24 hours

  • Redness, warmth, swelling, or pain is getting worse after 24‚Äì48 hours.
  • Pus, bad smell, or delayed healing.
  • You have diabetes, circulation problems, or immune issues.
  • You may need a tetanus booster (last shot >10 years, or >5 years with a dirty wound).
