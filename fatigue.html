Fatigue at Sea

Protect sleep you can get, use naps and caffeine as tools, and call out safety risks.

Purpose

Practical steps to stay alert on watches and avoid fatigue-related mishaps.

Jump straight to the section you need.

Quick steps Ongoing care Avoid References
Quick Steps Immediate reset for safer alertness
  • Protect one 4+ hour core sleep block each 24 hours whenever possible.
  • Take a 10-30 minute nap if you are nodding off or missing details.
  • Avoid caffeine in the 4-6 hours before planned sleep.
  • Tell your supervisor if you are too tired to be safe for current tasks.
Why this is worse at sea
  • Rotating watches, split sleep, and non-24-hour routines.
  • Noise, motion, and light leakage in berthing.
  • Heat/cold stress and high operational tempo.
Ongoing Care Build a repeatable sleep routine
  • Use the same short wind-down before rack time: wash, stretch, dim screens, and quiet the berth.
  • Use earplugs or white noise and an eye mask; keep rack courtesy to protect everyone's sleep.
  • Plan caffeine as a tool: smaller doses early in watch; about 400 mg/day or less for most healthy adults.
  • Hydrate regularly. Lighter meals/snacks support sleep better than heavy late meals.
  • If possible, pair a short nap with caffeine ("coffee nap"): drink, then nap 15-20 minutes.
  • Get bright light early in your wake period and dim light before sleep to support circadian rhythm.
Things to Avoid Protect alertness and reaction time
  • Stacking large caffeine doses late in your duty day.
  • Bright screens or intense gaming right before trying to sleep.
  • Skipping ear/eye protection in noisy, bright berthing.
  • Pushing through microsleeps instead of taking a short reset.
  • Using alcohol to force sleep; it fragments rest and worsens recovery.
Signs to be aware of
  • Microsleeps, nodding off, or missing calls and alarms.
  • Repeated mistakes on tasks you usually perform well.
  • Trouble tracking instructions or slower reactions on ladders/decks.
