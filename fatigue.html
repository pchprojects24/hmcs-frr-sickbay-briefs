Fatigue at Sea

Protect sleep you can get, use naps and caffeine as tools, and call out safety risks.

Purpose

Practical steps to stay alert on watches and avoid fatigue-related mishaps.

Try this now (60 seconds)

Immediate steps

  • Protect one 4+ hour core sleep block each 24 hours.
  • Take a 10–30 minute power nap if you are nodding off.
  • Avoid caffeine in the 4–6 hours before planned sleep.
  • Tell your supervisor if you are too tired to be safe.
Why this is worse at sea
  • Rotating watches, split sleep, and non-24-hour schedules.
  • Noise, motion, and light leakage in berthing and workspaces.
  • Heat or cold in compartments and high operational tempo.

Self-care for the next 24–72 hours

Build a small routine

  • Same quick wind-down before rack time: wash, stretch, dim screens, and quiet your space.
  • Use earplugs/white noise and an eye mask to block noise and light; keep rack courtesy for others.
  • Plan caffeine as a tool: small doses earlier in the watch; aim for about 400 mg/day or less for most healthy adults.
  • Drink water regularly; light meals and snacks are easier on sleep than heavy late meals.
  • If possible, pair a short nap with caffeine ("coffee nap"): drink, then nap 15–20 minutes so caffeine kicks in as you wake.

Avoid / common mistakes

Protect your alertness

  • Stacking large caffeine doses late in your duty day.
  • Bright screens or intense gaming right before trying to sleep.
  • Skipping ear protection/eye masks in noisy, bright berthing.
  • Pushing through microsleeps instead of taking a brief reset.
Safety red flags
  • Microsleeps, nodding off, or missing calls/alarms.
  • Repeated mistakes on tasks you know well.
  • Trouble tracking instructions or slower reactions on ladders/decks.

If you hit these, inform your supervisor and adjust duties/rest right away.

Go now (urgent / same watch)
  • Near-miss or safety incident linked to fatigue.
  • Fainting, chest pain, shortness of breath, or confusion.
Not improving (within 24–72 hours)
  • Persistent exhaustion despite naps and caffeine timing.
  • New headaches, mood changes, or sleep that never feels restorative.
  • Snoring with choking pauses reported by bunkmates.
