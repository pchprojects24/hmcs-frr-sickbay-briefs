Foot Care at Sea

Manage wet boots, blisters, and athlete's foot while keeping watch-ready.

For sailors dealing with damp boots, athlete's foot, blisters and "trench-foot-like" problems. This page covers daily prevention, self-care for common foot issues, and when to see Sick Bay.

Quick take

Common foot problems in boots

Dry socks, dry boots, and a 30-second check of your feet every day will prevent most problems. Don't ignore deep cracks, spreading redness or pain that makes it hard to stand watch — those need Sick Bay.

Long days in damp socks and boots can lead to:

  • Maceration (“soggy feet”) — pale, soft, wrinkled skin that tears and cracks easily.
  • Athlete’s foot (tinea pedis) — itchy, burning, peeling skin, especially between the toes.
  • Blisters and hot spots — friction bubbles on heels, toes and balls of the feet.
  • Calluses and corns — thick, hard skin or small hard bumps from ongoing rubbing or tight boots.
  • “Trench-foot-like” problems — cold + wet exposure causing numb, painful, swollen feet.
  • Secondary infections — redness, swelling or pus when germs enter through cracked skin.
Not covered here: major trauma, fractures, severe diabetic foot problems or deep ulcers. Those are emergencies — go straight to Sick Bay.
At a glance

Quick habits that make the biggest difference

  • Fresh socks every day — and change again mid-day on long watches.
  • Wash and dry your feet daily, especially between the toes.
  • Wear shower sandals in communal heads/showers.
  • Air boots and remove insoles after watch so they fully dry.
  • Use foot powder before problems start, not just after.
  • Pad & tape hot spots before they become blisters.
  • See Sick Bay if athlete’s foot doesn’t improve, or if there is spreading redness, pain or pus.
Why it happens

Why feet break down in damp boots

Feet inside boots are warm, dark and often wet with sweat. That combination causes:

  • Soft, fragile skin: when skin stays wet, it turns white and wrinkled and tears more easily (maceration).
  • Fungal overgrowth: fungus that causes athlete’s foot thrives in warm, moist toe webs and damp socks.
  • More blisters: wet skin rubs and shears more, so you blister faster in damp socks.
  • Cold-wet injury: in cold conditions, wet feet lose heat quickly and blood flow drops, which can damage tissues (“trench-foot-like” problems).
  • Entry points for infection: cracked heels, split toe webs and popped blisters give bacteria a way in.
Prevention

Daily prevention: socks, boots & hygiene

1. Socks

  • Use wool-blend or moisture-wicking socks; avoid 100% cotton that holds sweat.
  • New pair every day without fail. On a long duty day, change socks once in the middle as well.
  • Always put on clean, dry socks after a shower — not yesterday’s pair.
  • Avoid socks that are so tight they leave deep marks (reduced circulation) or so loose they bunch up and rub.

2. Boots

  • If you have two pairs, rotate boots so each pair can dry fully between uses.
  • After watch, open laces, remove insoles and air boots in a dry, ventilated spot.
  • Don’t sleep in boots or wet socks. Give feet an off-duty “airing out” every night.
  • Make sure boots fit properly: not crushing toes, not so loose your heel lifts and rubs.

3. Foot hygiene

  • Wash feet daily with soap, including between toes.
  • Dry thoroughly — towel between each toe before socks go on.
  • Wear shower sandals in communal showers and heads.
  • Keep toenails trimmed straight across; don’t share towels or footwear.

4. Moisture control

  • Use foot powder before a long watch:
    • Plain drying powder for sweaty feet.
    • Antifungal powder (e.g., tolnaftate, miconazole) if you tend to athlete’s foot.
  • Apply a light dusting to dry feet and inside socks/boots; avoid thick clumps.
  • Whenever possible on breaks, take boots and socks off for 5–10 minutes of air time.
Self-care

Early athlete’s foot: what you can do yourself

Typical signs:

  • Itchy, burning skin between toes.
  • Peeling or scaly patches, sometimes white and soggy in the toe webs.
  • Dry, flaky skin on the soles that doesn’t just look like “normal dry skin.”
  • Sometimes blisters or a strong foot odour.

Over-the-counter antifungals (ingredients)

At a pharmacy (or provided by Sick Bay), look for creams or sprays containing:

  • Clotrimazole 1%
  • Miconazole 2%
  • Terbinafine 1%

These all work similarly when used correctly.

How to use them

  • After showering, dry your feet well, especially between the toes.
  • Apply a thin layer of cream to the rash and 1–2 cm beyond the edges.
  • Use once or twice daily as per product instructions.
  • Continue treatment for 1–2 weeks after the rash clears to fully kill the fungus.
  • At the same time, tighten up hygiene: fresh socks, powder, shower sandals, and cleaning the shower floor where possible.
Important: cream alone won’t work if your feet stay wet in the same socks all day. The combination of medicine + dry feet + clean socks/boots is what cures it.
Sick Bay note: Athlete’s foot is a valid reason to see Sick Bay. Clotrimazole cream is available and can be issued with clear instructions if you’re not improving with your own measures.
Blisters & soggy feet

Blisters and maceration (“soggy feet”)

Blisters – quick rules

  • Small, not too painful: leave it intact. Pad around it (moleskin donut, blister plaster) and protect inside your sock.
  • Large or very painful: you can carefully drain it:
    • Clean skin; use a clean/sterilized needle to pierce near the edge.
    • Gently press out fluid; keep the “roof” skin in place.
    • Apply a small amount of ointment and cover with a non-stick dressing.
  • Do not tear off the overlying skin; it’s your natural bandage.
  • Watch for infection: increasing redness, pus, warmth or red streaks — those need Sick Bay.

Maceration (“soggy white” feet)

  • Remove wet socks/boots and gently dry feet as soon as you can.
  • Air feet in a warm, dry space. Don’t rub hard; the wet skin is fragile.
  • Once mostly dry, you may:
    • Use a little barrier ointment (e.g., petroleum jelly) on heels/soles if they crack easily.
    • Use powder, not ointment, between toes to keep that area dry.
  • If skin peels, trim only loose dead skin; don’t pull at it and don’t leave raw areas unprotected.
If maceration is severe (very painful, swollen, or you’ve lost sensation) or keeps coming back even after better habits, speak with Sick Bay. It may be early immersion foot or need stronger measures.
When to see Sick Bay

Red flags – don’t self-manage these

See Sick Bay promptly if:

  • Athlete’s foot isn’t improving after 2–4 weeks of proper cream + hygiene.
  • You have painful heel or toe cracks that make it hard to walk or wear boots.
  • There is spreading redness, warmth, swelling or pus around any broken skin.
  • You notice red streaks going up your foot or leg, or you feel feverish/unwell.
  • You have a very large or deep blister in a weight-bearing area that you can’t protect on your own.
  • Your feet are persistently numb, pale/blue, or very painful after cold-wet exposure.
  • Foot problems are interfering with your ability to safely stand watch, climb ladders or wear required footwear.
Get urgent help (don’t wait for routine Sick Parade) if:
  • Redness and swelling are spreading quickly.
  • You have severe pain, fever, or feel very unwell.
  • The foot looks dusky, purple or black, or you’re losing sensation.

Medical staff can provide stronger antifungals or antibiotics when needed, assess for immersion foot, and arrange modified duties or different footwear until your feet recover. Early visits mean easier fixes.
