Feet / Prevention

Foot Care at Sea

Dry feet, early hotspot care, and quick action to avoid blisters or infection.

Purpose

Keep feet comfortable and safe for long watches in damp boots.

Try this now (60 seconds)

Immediate steps

  • Change into dry socks; dry between toes.
  • Air boots and insoles away from direct heat.
  • Check for hotspots; pad and protect them before they blister.
  • Gently dry any macerated (whitish, soft) skin.
Why this is worse at sea
  • Long watches in damp boots with limited drying time.
  • Sweat plus cold-wet exposure soften skin and cause breakdown.
  • Gear friction and ladders turn small hotspots into blisters fast.

Self-care for the next 24–72 hours

Protect skin and nails

  • Rotate socks through drying; use thin liner socks if prone to blisters.
  • Apply petroleum jelly or blister tape to hotspots before watch.
  • If spray/immersion is likely, use a waterproof dressing over clean skin and change once dry.
  • Keep toe webs dry; remove boots when safe to air feet during down time.

Avoid / common mistakes

Small issues grow fast

  • Ignoring hotspots—treat them before they blister.
  • Staying in wet socks through a whole watch.
  • Using tight tape or wraps that cut off circulation.
Hotspot → blister micro-protocol
  • Stop, dry the area, and change socks.
  • Pad with moleskin/blister dressing; ensure no wrinkles.
  • Re-check halfway through the next watch.
Athlete's foot basics
  • Keep toe webs dry and change socks often.
  • Use an over-the-counter antifungal cream as directed; keep using for a few days after the rash clears.
  • Do not share shower shoes, towels, or nail tools.
Immersion foot warning

Prolonged cold-wet exposure with numb, tingling, pale, or mottled skin—get evaluated.

Higher-risk feet: diabetes, poor circulation, or nerve issues mean lower threshold to see Sick Bay, especially with any sign of infection.
Go now (urgent / same watch)
  • Rapid swelling, spreading redness, fever, or streaks up the foot/leg.
  • Severe pain, numbness, or color change after cold-wet exposure.
Not improving (within 24–72 hours)
  • Blisters that keep refilling or won't heal.
  • Athlete's foot not settling after a week of treatment.
  • Cracks between toes that look red, ooze, or block wearing boots.
