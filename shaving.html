Overview

What‚Äôs going on with my skin?

Razor burn (irritation)

  • Red, burning or stinging skin right after shaving.
  • Caused by friction, dry shaving, dull blades, or pressing too hard.
  • Looks like a red rash more than separate ‚Äúpimples‚Äù.

Razor bumps (pseudofolliculitis barbae / PFB)

  • Ingrown hairs ‚Äì small firm bumps, often with a hair trapped inside.
  • Common on the neck and jawline, especially with curly or coarse hair.
  • Not mainly an infection, but bumps can become infected if picked or shaved over.

Folliculitis (infected hair follicles)

  • Bacteria get into irritated or cut hair follicles.
  • Small, sore or itchy pimples with a white or yellow head.
  • Can merge into larger, painful areas or deeper lumps if not treated.

Other shaving issues

  • Red, itchy rash from products (contact dermatitis).
  • Dry, tight, itchy skin from hot water, soap, and daily shaving.
  • Shaving aggravating acne if you cut through pimples.
  • Dark marks or raised scars after repeated irritation.

Quick Wins

Fast changes that usually help

‚úÖ DO

  • Shave after a warm shower or warm, wet towel.
  • Always use shaving cream or gel ‚Äì never dry shave.
  • Shave with the grain, especially on the neck.
  • Use light pressure and short strokes; let the blade do the work.
  • Rinse with cool water after shaving.
  • Apply a gentle moisturizer or aftershave balm.
  • Change blades regularly; keep razors clean and dry.
  • Consider single-blade or electric options if you get a lot of bumps.

‚õî DON‚ÄôT

  • Don‚Äôt shave against the grain if you get razor bumps.
  • Don‚Äôt pull or stretch the skin tight while shaving.
  • Don‚Äôt go over the same spot again and again without re-lathering.
  • Don‚Äôt use harsh, alcohol-heavy aftershaves on stingy, dry skin.
  • Don‚Äôt pick, squeeze, or dig at ingrown hairs or pustules.
  • Don‚Äôt share razors.

Routine

Better shave routine (step by step)

Step 1 ‚Äì Before you shave

  • Warm up: shave after a warm shower or hold a warm, wet cloth on the area for 2‚Äì3 minutes.
  • Clean the skin: wash with a mild face wash or gentle soap (not a harsh detergent bar).
  • Lather up: apply shaving cream/gel/soap and let it sit for a minute; use a shaving brush if you have one to gently lift hairs.

Step 2 ‚Äì During the shave

  • Direction: shave with the grain. Check with your fingers which way the hair lies flat.
  • Pressure & strokes: use light pressure and short strokes; rinse the blade every few strokes.
  • Number of passes: try to shave each area once. If you must go again, re-apply lather first.
  • How close is ‚Äúclose enough‚Äù: aim for ‚Äúinspection clean,‚Äù not glass-smooth. If allowed, leaving about 1 mm of stubble often reduces ingrowns.

Step 3 ‚Äì After the shave

  • Rinse and cool: rinse with cool water or use a cool, damp cloth for a minute.
  • Moisturize: apply an alcohol-free, fragrance-free moisturizer or aftershave balm.
  • Hands off: don‚Äôt scratch or pick at bumps, even if they itch.

Tools

Shaving gear ‚Äì what to consider

Multi-blade cartridges

  • Pros: fast, very close shave.
  • Cons: more friction; higher risk of ingrown hairs, especially with curly/coarse hair.
  • Use very light pressure, shave with the grain, and change cartridges often.

Single-blade razors

  • Safety razors or single-blade disposables.
  • Pros: cut at skin level without ‚Äúlift and cut‚Äù; often better for bump-prone skin.
  • Cons: small learning curve; still need gentle technique.

Electric shavers / clippers

  • Foil or rotary shavers; beard trimmers with guards.
  • Pros: usually leave a tiny bit of stubble ‚Üí fewer ingrowns; quick.
  • Cons: not baby-smooth; need power and regular cleaning.

Chemical depilatories

  • Creams or powders that dissolve hair.
  • Pros: no blade; hair regrows with a blunt tip.
  • Cons: can irritate or burn if misused; strong smell; must follow directions exactly.

Waxing or plucking beard hairs is generally not recommended ‚Äì it can cause more irritation and ingrown hairs.

Self-Care

Your shaving self-care kit

Things you can buy yourself and keep in your wash bag or locker:

  • Gentle face wash: mild, non-scrubbing cleanser for daily use.
  • Shaving lubricant: unscented or ‚Äúsensitive skin‚Äù shaving cream, gel, or soap.
  • Good razor or electric: whatever you choose, keep blades sharp and clean.
  • Moisturizer / aftershave balm: alcohol-free, fragrance-free if your skin is reactive.

Optional extras if you get a lot of bumps:

  • Mild chemical exfoliant (salicylic or glycolic acid pads/lotions) a few times a week to help prevent trapped hairs.
  • Benzoyl peroxide face wash once daily if you get ‚Äúpimple-like‚Äù folliculitis.
  • OTC 0.5‚Äì1% hydrocortisone cream for short-term use on a bad patch of razor burn or bumps (a few days max).
  • Warm compress routine: warm, wet cloth on sore bumps for 5‚Äì10 minutes, then gentle cleanse and moisturize.

Start new products slowly and one at a time so you can tell what helps (or irritates) your skin.

When to see medical

When to come to Sick Bay

See Sick Bay or a MO if:

  • The shaved area is getting more red, hot, swollen, or very painful.
  • You have lots of pus-filled spots or a deep, painful lump.
  • Razor bumps are severe, spreading, or starting to scar.
  • Shaving problems mean you cannot shave to standard without bleeding or significant pain.
  • You‚Äôve tried careful shaving and basic self-care for a few weeks and things are not improving.
  • You‚Äôre not sure it‚Äôs just shaving rash or something else.

What medical staff might do:

  • Confirm whether it‚Äôs mainly razor burn, PFB, folliculitis, acne, or another condition.
  • Prescribe stronger creams or gels (e.g. antibiotic, retinoid, short-term steroid) if needed.
  • Treat or prevent infection with topical or short-course oral antibiotics when appropriate.
  • Talk with you about alternative shaving tools suited to your skin.
  • For severe or recurrent cases, consider referral for procedures (e.g. laser hair reduction) or a temporary shaving profile so your skin can heal.

Shaving should be routine, not something that injures you every day. If it is, talk to Sick Bay early rather than waiting for scars or bigger problems.
