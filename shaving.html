Shaving & Razor Bumps

Prevent razor burn, bumps, and irritation with better technique and simple self-care.

For sailors who have to shave for work, especially if you get red skin, small bumps, or "pimples" on the lower cheeks or neck. This page covers what causes razor irritation, practical self-care, and when to see Sick Bay.

Quick take

What's going on with my skin?

All three problems — razor burn, bumps, and folliculitis — can overlap. The aim here is to calm the skin, reduce bumps, and keep you able to shave safely for work.

Razor burn (irritation)

  • Red, burning or stinging skin right after shaving.
  • From friction, dry shaving, dull blades, or pressing too hard.

Razor bumps (ingrown hairs / pseudofolliculitis)

  • Small firm bumps, often with a hair trapped inside, usually on the neck or jawline.
  • More common with curly or coarse hair and very close shaves.

Folliculitis (infected follicles)

  • Bacteria get into irritated hair follicles.
  • Small sore pimples with white/yellow heads that can crust.
Quick wins

Do's & Don'ts

Fast changes that usually help quickly.

✅ DO

  • Shave after a warm shower.
  • Always use shaving cream or gel (never dry shave).
  • Shave with the grain, especially on the neck.
  • Use light pressure and short strokes.
  • Rinse with cool water after shaving.
  • Use clean, sharp blades and replace regularly.

⛔ DON'T

  • Don't shave against the grain.
  • Don't stretch skin tight while shaving.
  • Don't go over the same spot again and again without re-lathering.
  • Don't use strong alcohol-heavy aftershaves that sting and dry you out.
  • Don't pick or squeeze bumps or ingrown hairs.
Step-by-step

Routine for Better Shaving

Step 1 – Before you shave

  • Warm up: shave after a warm shower or hold a warm, wet cloth on the area for 2–3 minutes.
  • Clean the skin: wash with a mild, non-scrubby face wash.
  • Lather up: apply shaving cream/gel and let it sit for about a minute to soften hairs.

Step 2 – During the shave

  • Direction: shave with the grain. Check which way hair grows in each area.
  • Pressure: use light pressure and short strokes; let the blade do the work.
  • Passes: try to shave each area once. If you must go again, rinse and re-lather first.
  • Aim: aim for "inspection clean," not glass-smooth. Leaving ~1 mm of stubble usually causes fewer ingrowns (follow unit shaving standards or medical profile).

Step 3 – After the shave

  • Rinse and cool: rinse with cool water, then gently pat dry.
  • Moisturize: apply a gentle, alcohol-free moisturizer or balm.
  • Hands off: avoid scratching, rubbing, or picking bumps.
Shaving gear

Choosing the Right Tools

Multi-blade cartridges

  • Pros: fast, very close shave.
  • Cons: more friction and "lift and cut" can cause more ingrown hairs, especially on curly hair.
  • Tip: use very light pressure and don't chase an ultra-close shave if you get bumps.

Single-blade (safety) razors

  • Pros: cuts hairs at skin level; often better for bump-prone skin.
  • Cons: needs a bit of practice to use safely.

Electric shavers / clippers

  • Pros: usually leave a tiny amount of stubble, which reduces ingrown hairs; quick.
  • Cons: not baby-smooth; needs power and regular cleaning.
  • Note: any stubble length must still meet grooming/respirator standards for your role.

Chemical depilatory creams

  • Pros: no blade; hair regrows with a blunt end.
  • Cons: can irritate or burn skin and often smell strong; must follow package directions exactly and patch-test first.
Self-care kit

What to Keep in Your Wash Bag

Useful items for your wash bag (ingredient-based, not brand-specific):

  • Gentle face wash: mild, non-scrub cleanser for daily use.
  • Shaving lubricant: shaving cream/gel for sensitive skin (avoid strong fragrance if you react easily).
  • Moisturizer: simple alcohol-free moisturizer or aftershave balm.

Optional extras (over-the-counter)

  • Mild chemical exfoliant: product with a low strength of salicylic acid or similar, used a few times per week to help prevent trapped hairs. Stop if it burns or peels too much.
  • Benzoyl peroxide wash: a low-strength wash once daily if you get pimple-like folliculitis; rinse off well and be aware it can bleach fabric.
  • Hydrocortisone cream (0.5–1%): for short-term use (a few days) on bad razor burn to reduce redness and itch. Not for daily, long-term use.

Start new products slowly and one at a time so you know what your skin tolerates.

Escalation

When to see medical

  • Bumps are very painful, keep getting infected, or you see pus, crusting, or spreading redness.
  • You have many deep, hard bumps or scars on the neck or jawline.
  • Rash is blistering, oozing, or keeping you awake at night.
  • Skin problems make it hard to shave to required standards, wear PPE, or do your job safely.
  • You've tried good shaving technique and basic self-care for a few weeks and things are not improving or are getting worse.
  • You're unsure if it's shaving rash, infection, eczema, or something else.

Medical staff can check for infection or other skin conditions and talk through options such as different shaving methods, medicated creams, or (if needed) shaving profiles in line with policy.
